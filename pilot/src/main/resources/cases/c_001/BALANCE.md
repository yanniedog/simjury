## Argument for guilt

The Crown's case is that one man, posing as a wealthy gentleman, defrauded a series of respectable
women by an identical method, and that the accused is that man.

The method is consistent across complainants who did not know one another: he presented himself as
titled or well-connected, wrote out a list of dresses and jewellery to be ordered on his account,
took rings "for sizing," and left a worthless cheque (T-W01-001, T-W01-003, T-W03-002, X-01, X-02,
X-04). Four independent women each identified the accused as that caller after the event
(T-W01-005, T-W03-005, T-W04-005). The clothing lists and cheques are real documents written in a
disguised hand, and the handwriting expert, comparing them, attributed them to the accused
(T-W06-002, T-W06-003, X-03). When his lodgings were searched, police found white and brown spats
and other fine articles matching the complainants' descriptions of the caller (T-W05-005, X-08). A
retired constable produced a certificate of an earlier conviction for an identical fraud and named
the accused as that same man (T-W07-002, T-W07-003). The identification parade was conducted and
recorded (X-06, T-W05-002), and the schedule of encounters shows a coherent campaign over months
(X-05). Taken together — a repeated method, multiple independent identifications, documentary
handwriting, incriminating possessions, and a proven history of the very same fraud — the evidence
points to a single deliberate fraudster. The accused offers only denial. On this record the Crown
says the jury can be sure the accused made the pretences charged, and that each complainant parted
with property because of them.

## Argument for acquittal

The defence says the Crown has proved a fraud, but not the identity of the fraudster, and that the
identifications are unreliable.

Every complainant saw the caller only briefly, and each conceded the sighting was compromised. Mrs
Elling read newspaper reports of a man "charged with similar frauds" before she ever attended to
identify anyone (T-W01-006); complainants attended the police court repeatedly and heard one
another give evidence (T-W01-008); and at the parade the first witness hesitated yet went in first,
setting the pattern for those who followed (T-W03-008). Even Inspector Vale, in charge of the case,
would not treat identification in the dock as sufficient without independent support, and noticed
the women "seemed to feel sympathy" (T-W05-003, T-W05-004). The handwriting is equivocal: the fraud
documents were written "with freedom," whereas those who deal with the accused daily say he writes
slowly and badly, with a peculiar grip (T-W06-005, T-W08-006) — the expert's case depends on
assuming disguise (K-03). Crucially, the prior conviction cuts the other way: the defence sought to
show that the man convicted long ago was imprisoned during the period of some of these very frauds
(T-W07-005, T-W07-006). Inspector Vale had himself inquired after another person known for passing
bad cheques by similar pretences (T-W05-007), and no complainant's property was ever found on the
accused (T-W04-007). The chronology exhibit exposes a genuine tension the Crown cannot resolve
(X-07). Confident identification of a stranger months later, primed by newspapers and shared
exposure, is exactly what the identification caution warns against (D-02). On this record the jury
cannot be sure.
